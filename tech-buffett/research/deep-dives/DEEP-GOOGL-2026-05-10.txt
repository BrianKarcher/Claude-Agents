0. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
This deep dive is structured around the seven marching orders the initiation
left behind, plus 10-year scenarios, a thesis-break checklist, and DCF
marching orders.

Headline conclusions:

  (1) Q1 2026 Cloud margin step-up to 32.9% (from 17.8% YoY) is dominantly
      structural -- ~70-80% of the 1,510bps move is operating leverage
      against a now-significant fixed-cost base, with the residual being
      Wiz revenue mix, FX, and a TPU-supply-deal contribution that begins
      flowing later in 2026/2027. Steady-state Cloud OM by FY28 likely
      30-33% in our base case, with upside to 35-36% if TPU monetization
      and Workspace AI attach hit. This is the single most important
      finding in the deep dive.

  (2) Antitrust scenario tree (Search + Ad Tech) probability-weights to
      a $9-12B annual EBIT impairment by FY28-FY30, with a tail risk of
      $30B+ in the Chrome-divestiture/Ad-Manager-structural-breakup case
      (~6% combined probability). The CURRENT MULTIPLE ALREADY PRICES
      ROUGHLY ~$15B/yr of antitrust drag -- so the base case is modestly
      anti-thetical. The tail is the real risk, and it is bounded.

  (3) Capex normalization curve: FY25 $91B -> FY26 $145-155B (our point
      estimate $148B, anchored to $35.7B Q1 actual + accelerating run-
      rate) -> FY27 $145-160B (still scaling) -> FY28-29 $130-145B
      (plateau as TPU/data center pipeline catches up to demand) -> FY30
      $115-125B (normalization). This is ~50% higher than the
      initiation's 4-quarter-supercycle prior. The $462B Cloud RPO and
      "multi-gigawatt TPU supply agreements" disclosed in the 10-Q
      indicate the demand is real and contracted -- not speculative
      build-ahead.

  (4) Search TAM under AI substitution: base case Search rev grows 8-10%
      CAGR through FY30 (down from 11-13% historical 5yr CAGR), with AI
      Overviews + AI Mode acting as MONETIZABLE NEW SURFACES, not pure
      cannibalization. The Q1 print (+19% Search rev growth, paid clicks
      +6%, CPC +7%) is the strongest empirical refutation of the "Search
      is dying" bear thesis we have. Bear case: 4-5% CAGR; bull case:
      11-13% CAGR (subscription mix shift accelerates).

  (5) Capital allocation under Ashkenazi: the Q1 buyback pause is a
      sequencing artifact (M&A close + $31B notes issuance + capex peak
      in same quarter), NOT a structural shift. With $69.5B remaining
      authorization and our base-case FY26 SBC-adj FCF estimate of
      ~$50-65B, expect $35-50B of buybacks in Q2-Q4 2026 + dividend
      growth ~5%/yr. Buyback pace recovers to ~$45-55B annual run-rate
      by FY27. This is a meaningful per-share IV lever.

  (6) Waymo SOTP: $80-120B fair value range. 500K rides/wk = ~26M
      annualized; at $20-25 implied gross revenue per ride and ~50%
      AV-platform take, ~$2.5-3.5B FY26 revenue at meaningful loss.
      Sum-of-parts at 8-12x FY30 normalized revenue (assumed $15-25B)
      = $120-300B; we discount heavily for execution risk and net at
      ~$100B base. Adds ~$8/share to GOOGL valuation in our base case
      and ~$25/share in bull. NOT in DCF base case; treated as
      optionality.

  (7) Cloud margin trajectory vs. AWS/Azure: GCP's 32.9% Q1 OM is now
      just 4-5pts below AWS's mid-30s and likely AT-OR-AHEAD of Azure's
      Cloud-only OM (Microsoft does not disclose Azure margin
      separately; estimated ~30-35%). The gap to AWS will likely close
      to <2pts by FY28 because GCP has ~80% of the same scale advantages
      with 100% of the in-house silicon advantages (TPU, Gemini). GCP
      OM in steady state should be 33-37% by FY30, comparable to AWS.
      This finding alone repositions GOOGL from a "discount-to-MSFT
      story" to an "AWS-quality cloud business at MSFT's multiple"
      story.

10-YEAR SCENARIOS (probability-weighted, summary):

1. Q1 2026 CLOUD MARGIN STEP-UP DECOMPOSITION
================================================================================

The most consequential single data point in the GOOGL story is Google
Cloud's Q1 2026 operating margin: 32.9%, up from 17.8% in Q1 2025. A
1,510bps YoY step-up in a hyperscaler segment in a single quarter is
unusual enough to demand careful decomposition.

1.1 THE RAW NUMBERS

  +-----------------------+-----------+-----------+-----------+---------+
  | Cloud line            | Q1 2025   | Q1 2026   | YoY $     | YoY %   |
  +-----------------------+-----------+-----------+-----------+---------+
  | Revenue               | $12,260M  | $20,028M  | +$7,768M  | +63%    |
  | Operating income      | $2,177M   | $6,598M   | +$4,421M  | +203%   |
  | Operating margin      | 17.8%     | 32.9%     | +1,510bps | n/m     |
  | Cost of segment       | $10,083M  | $13,430M  | +$3,347M  | +33%    |
  +-----------------------+-----------+-----------+-----------+---------+

  Implied incremental margin: $4,421M / $7,768M = **57%**

  An incremental margin of 57% on a hyperscaler business is striking.
  AWS at maturity has cycled between 35-50% incremental margins
  depending on capex digestion. 57% suggests a substantial slice of the
  $7.8B incremental revenue dropped through with very little
  incremental variable cost.

1.2 DECOMPOSITION: WHAT EXPLAINS THE 1,510bps STEP-UP?

  We decompose the +1,510bps move into five buckets, with our best
  estimate of contribution and confidence level:

  +-------------------------------+--------+-----------+--------------+
  | Driver                        | Est bps| % of move | Confidence   |
  +-------------------------------+--------+-----------+--------------+
  | (A) Operating leverage on a   | +900   | ~60%      | HIGH         |
  |     newly-large fixed cost    |        |           |              |
  |     base                      |        |           |              |
  | (B) Mix shift to higher-      | +250   | ~16%      | MEDIUM-HIGH  |
  |     margin AI compute /       |        |           |              |
  |     Workspace + Vertex pull-  |        |           |              |
  |     through                   |        |           |              |
  | (C) Wiz acquisition close     | +150   | ~10%      | MEDIUM       |
  |     (closed March 11, 2026 so |        |           |              |
  |     ~3 weeks of consolidated  |        |           |              |
  |     contribution)             |        |           |              |
  | (D) FX tailwind on largely    | +100   | ~7%       | MEDIUM       |
  |     USD-denominated revenue   |        |           |              |
  | (E) One-time / non-recurring  | +110   | ~7%       | LOW (gap)    |
  |     items (release of accruals|        |           |              |
  |     etc.) - speculative since |        |           |              |
  |     no transcript on file     |        |           |              |
  +-------------------------------+--------+-----------+--------------+
  | Total                         | +1,510 | 100%      |              |
  +-------------------------------+--------+-----------+--------------+

  Comment on each bucket:

  (A) OPERATING LEVERAGE (~900bps, 60% of step-up). Cloud's segment cost
      growth was 33% YoY ($3.3B incremental cost on $7.8B incremental
      revenue). Once a hyperscaler reaches the scale where data-center
      depreciation, salaries, and energy are largely fixed against a
      revenue line that is growing at 60%+, the incremental margin
      flips to majority-flow-through. AWS hit this inflection in 2018-
      2019 (segment OM went from ~20% to ~28% in 18 months). GCP
      reaching it now -- at $80B annualized run-rate, similar to where
      AWS was in 2018 -- is consistent with the AWS playbook.

  (B) MIX SHIFT (~250bps, 16%). Three sub-drivers:
      - Vertex/Gemini Enterprise: API-priced compute with no human-
        delivery component, structurally higher margin than compute
        rental.
      - Workspace AI: Gemini for Workspace adds ~$10/user/month at
        near-zero incremental delivery cost on top of paying Workspace
        users -- almost pure-margin uplift.
      - Backlog mix: $462B Cloud RPO is dominated by multi-year
        enterprise commitments which have higher reservation pricing
        and lower customer-acquisition cost amortization per dollar of
        revenue.

  (C) WIZ INTEGRATION (~150bps, 10%). Wiz closed March 11, 2026, so
      Q1 captured ~21 days of consolidated contribution. Wiz is a
      cloud security platform with software gross margins (~80%+) and
      ARR of ~$700M at close. ~$45M of Wiz revenue at high contribution
      margin is consistent with ~150bps lift on the $20B Cloud
      segment. NOTE: in steady state Wiz's full-quarter contribution
      will continue to add ~30-50bps for the next 3 quarters before
      lapping.

  (D) FX (~100bps, 7%). Cloud revenue is largely USD-denominated for
      enterprise contracts, but international cost base (non-US
      headcount, non-US data center energy) dropped marginally on a
      stronger USD. Modest tailwind only.

  (E) ONE-TIME / UNDISCLOSED (~110bps, 7%). Without a transcript, we
      cannot be precise. Possible sources: release of legal/disputed-
      revenue accruals, true-ups on prior-period data center
      depreciation, change in lease accounting on the $75.6B not-yet-
      commenced data center leases (which begin between 2026 and
      2031). This is the bucket most likely to reverse partially in
      Q2-Q4 2026.

1.3 STRESS TEST: IS THIS DURABLE?

  Three tests for durability:

  TEST 1 -- AWS analog. AWS segment OM 2018-2022:
    +------+------+------+------+------+------+
    | 2018 | 2019 | 2020 | 2021 | 2022 | 2023 |
    +------+------+------+------+------+------+
    | 28%  | 26%  | 30%  | 30%  | 28%  | 26%  |
    +------+------+------+------+------+------+
  AWS held in a 26-30% band for 5 years post-inflection, with the band
  narrowing in capex ramp years (2022-23). GCP's 32.9% Q1 print is at
  the top of this band but plausibly within. GCP has a structural
  margin advantage from in-house TPU silicon (no NVIDIA gross-margin
  share-of-wallet) which AWS does not have; GCP has a structural
  margin disadvantage from smaller scale. Net we believe GCP can hold
  in a 30-35% band.

  TEST 2 -- Capex digestion. FY25 capex was $91.4B, ~22.7% of revenue.
  FY26 is heading to $145-155B, ~30% of revenue. Depreciation lags
  capex by ~3-4 years on data center assets. The depreciation flowing
  through Q1 2026's Cloud P&L was largely on FY22-FY23 capex ($23-32B
  pace). FY26-FY27 will see depreciation step up materially as the
  $91B FY25 capex flows through. Our base case: Cloud OM gives back
  100-200bps over FY26-FY27 as depreciation catches up, then re-
  expands as revenue continues to outgrow.

  TEST 3 -- Backlog rate of consumption. Cloud RPO is $462B, of which
  ~50% recognizes within 24 months = ~$230B. That implies $115B/year
  of pure-backlog Cloud revenue if all current backlog runs off
  evenly. Plus new bookings. Plus consumption beyond contracted
  minimums. The implied 24-month-out Cloud revenue is $130-145B
  annualized -- which is consistent with Cloud growing 50-60% YoY in
  FY27 from a ~$80B run-rate. This is a structural-revenue story; the
  margin can reasonably hold even with capex digestion drag.

1.4 STEADY-STATE CLOUD MARGIN ESTIMATE

  +---------------+----------+----------+----------+----------+
  | Year          | FY26E    | FY27E    | FY28E    | FY30E    |
  +---------------+----------+----------+----------+----------+
  | Cloud OM Bear | 26%      | 24%      | 25%      | 27%      |
  | Cloud OM Base | 31%      | 30%      | 31%      | 33%      |
  | Cloud OM Bull | 33%      | 33%      | 34%      | 36%      |
  +---------------+----------+----------+----------+----------+

  Q1'26 was 32.9%. Our base case is that the FY26 average comes in
  around 31% (some Q2-Q4 give-back to Q1) and re-accelerates over
  FY27-FY30 as revenue grows faster than the depreciation tail of the
  capex peak.

CONCLUSION: The Q1 2026 Cloud margin step-up is ~70-80% structural and
~20-30% Q1-specific. Steady-state Cloud OM in our base case is 30-33%,
materially above the 23.7% FY25 baseline. **This is the most important
finding in the deep dive**: it lifts our Cloud-segment FY30 operating
income estimate from ~$30-35B (initiation prior) to ~$45-55B (post deep
dive), an ~$15-20B annual EBIT delta that flows directly into the DCF.

2. ANTITRUST SCENARIO TREE (DOJ SEARCH + DOJ AD TECH)
================================================================================

Two pending matters dominate the antitrust risk:
  - DOJ Search (final judgment Dec 2025, on appeal both sides)
  - DOJ Ad Tech (April 2025 mixed liability ruling; remedies pending)

Plus secondary matters: Texas AG Ad Tech, EC enforcement under DMA,
private follow-on litigation. These are less central but additive.

2.1 DOJ SEARCH SCENARIO TREE

  Final judgment Dec 2025 included two key remedy categories: (a)
  restrictions on distribution agreements (the Apple TAC ~$20B/yr deal,
  Android default search), (b) data-sharing/syndication requirements
  with specified competitors. Remedies are STAYED pending appeal; both
  sides have appealed.

  We model 5 outcomes:

  +-----+-----------------------------+------+----------------+----------+
  | #   | Outcome                     | Prob | Annual EBIT $  | Stock $  |
  +-----+-----------------------------+------+----------------+----------+
  | S1  | Behavioral remedies only    | 25%  | -$2 to -$5B    | -2% to   |
  |     | (no Apple TAC change, only  |      |                | -4%      |
  |     | data-sharing).              |      |                |          |
  | S2  | Apple TAC reduced 30-50%    | 35%  | -$6 to -$10B   | -5% to   |
  |     | + data-sharing (BASE CASE)  |      | (TAC savings   | -8%      |
  |     |                             |      | partially      |          |
  |     |                             |      | offset by      |          |
  |     |                             |      | query loss)    |          |
  | S3  | Apple TAC fully prohibited  | 20%  | -$8 to -$15B   | -8% to   |
  |     | + Android default open      |      | (large query   | -12%     |
  |     | auction                     |      | share          |          |
  |     |                             |      | shift)         |          |
  | S4  | Above + Chrome behavioral   | 15%  | -$12 to -$20B  | -12% to  |
  |     | restrictions (no            |      |                | -18%     |
  |     | divestiture)                |      |                |          |
  | S5  | Chrome divestiture (TAIL)   | 5%   | -$25 to -$40B  | -20% to  |
  |     |                             |      |                | -30%     |
  +-----+-----------------------------+------+----------------+----------+

  Probability-weighted Search-only annual EBIT impact:
    0.25*-3.5 + 0.35*-8.0 + 0.20*-11.5 + 0.15*-16 + 0.05*-32.5
    = -$0.9 - $2.8 - $2.3 - $2.4 - $1.6 = **-$10.0B/year**

  Notes on construction:
    - The Apple TAC payment is currently estimated at ~$20-25B/yr.
      Eliminating it ENTIRELY is not unambiguously bad for GOOGL --
      the ~$20B saved offsets a significant portion of the lost search
      revenue (querying behavior on iOS would partially shift to
      ChatGPT/others, but Google would also stop paying $20B). The net
      EBIT hit is thus much smaller than the gross revenue at risk.
    - Data-sharing/syndication requirements are the more insidious
      remedy. They give competitors access to query data, meaning over
      5-10 years competitor relevance closes the gap. We model this as
      a slow drag (-50bps to -100bps to Search rev growth/year) rather
      than a step-change.
    - Chrome divestiture (S5) at 5% is calibrated based on (i) the
      District Court's reluctance to impose structural remedies (per
      Judge Mehta's stated philosophy in the August 2024 liability
      opinion), (ii) DOJ's mixed track record on Big Tech structural
      remedies, (iii) the appellate court's likely scrutiny of any
      structural divestiture order. Could be 3-7% in different
      observers' framing; we use 5%.

2.2 DOJ AD TECH SCENARIO TREE

  April 2025 mixed liability ruling: publisher-side tools (Google Ad
  Manager / DoubleClick for Publishers) found anticompetitive;
  advertiser-side tools cleared; DoubleClick + AdMeld acquisitions
  cleared. Remedy phase Sept-Nov 2025. Final judgment pending.

  We model 4 outcomes:

  +-----+-----------------------------+------+----------------+----------+
  | #   | Outcome                     | Prob | Annual EBIT $  | Stock $  |
  +-----+-----------------------------+------+----------------+----------+
  | A1  | Behavioral remedies only    | 35%  | -$1 to -$2B    | -1% to   |
  |     | (interoperability, data     |      |                | -2%      |
  |     | portability, must-carry     |      |                |          |
  |     | rivals)                     |      |                |          |
  | A2  | Behavioral + partial        | 35%  | -$2 to -$4B    | -2% to   |
  |     | unbundling (DFP separated   |      |                | -3%      |
  |     | as a business unit) (BASE)  |      |                |          |
  | A3  | Forced divestiture of DFP   | 25%  | -$4 to -$8B    | -4% to   |
  |     | (publisher-side)            |      |                | -7%      |
  | A4  | Full Ad Manager structural  | 5%   | -$8 to -$15B   | -7% to   |
  |     | breakup (advertiser + pub)  |      |                | -12%     |
  +-----+-----------------------------+------+----------------+----------+

  Probability-weighted Ad Tech annual EBIT impact:
    0.35*-1.5 + 0.35*-3.0 + 0.25*-6.0 + 0.05*-11.5
    = -$0.5 - $1.1 - $1.5 - $0.6 = **-$3.7B/year**

  Notes on construction:
    - Direct revenue at stake is Google Network ($29.8B FY25, declining
      ~2-4%/yr secular). Ad Manager publisher tools represent ~60-70%
      of this.
    - EBIT impact is much smaller than the gross revenue at risk
      because (a) Network is the lowest-margin part of the ad stack
      (much of the revenue passes through to publishers as TAC), (b)
      losing Ad Manager would also remove the associated TAC obligation,
      (c) advertiser-side tools (DV360) are unaffected and retain the
      higher-value enterprise advertiser relationships.
    - Texas AG case follows DOJ case sequentially; we add ~$0.5-1B/year
      for incremental Texas AG remedies on top.

2.3 COMBINED ANTITRUST EBIT IMPACT

  Search prob-wtd:    -$10.0B/year
  Ad Tech prob-wtd:    -$3.7B/year
  Texas AG / EC etc.:  -$1.0B/year
  ----------------------------------
  COMBINED:            -$14.7B/year by FY28-FY30

  This is at the upper end of the initiation's $6-15B range; the deep-
  dive analysis using explicit scenario trees lands closer to ~$15B
  than ~$10B. On a base-case FY28 operating income of ~$190B (16%
  CAGR off FY25 $129B with margin expansion), this represents ~7.7%
  of EBIT. **A noticeable hit, but not company-breaking.**

  Importantly, the market multiple appears to be pricing roughly this
  much already:
    - GOOGL trades at ~22-24x fwd P/E
    - MSFT at ~22-26x fwd P/E
    - META at ~22x fwd P/E
  GOOGL gets no multiple premium for being the largest, fastest-
  growing-cloud, most-profitable hyperscaler. Comparing to MSFT's
  multiple in particular suggests ~7-10% discount that maps to roughly
  $13-18B/yr EBIT haircut -- aligned with our $15B estimate.

  Implication for DCF: We model the antitrust hit as a ~$15B/year
  EBIT impairment beginning FY28, ramping linearly from $0 in FY25 to
  $15B in FY28. We do NOT add an additional multiple penalty because
  the impact is already in the FCF.

2.4 TAIL RISK FRAMING

  The combined tail (S5 Chrome divestiture AND A4 full Ad Manager
  breakup) is approximately 0.05*0.05 = 0.25% probability. The
  probability of EITHER tail event is ~9% (5% + 5% - 0.25%
  intersection). In that ~9% world, EBIT impact is $35-55B/yr, market
  cap impact 25-40% (-$1.0-1.6T). This is the bear-case scenario for
  GOOGL and the reason a margin-of-safety still matters even at this
  multiple.

3. CAPEX NORMALIZATION CURVE (FY25 $91B -> FY26 $145-155B -> ?)
================================================================================

The single biggest variable separating the bull and bear DCF cases is
the shape of the capex curve.

3.1 THE FACT BASE

  Historical capex:
  +-------+--------+----------+--------------------+
  | Year  | Capex  | % of rev | Annualized 4Q rate |
  +-------+--------+----------+--------------------+
  | 2022  | $31.5B | 11.1%    | $7.9B/qtr          |
  | 2023  | $32.3B | 10.5%    | $8.1B/qtr          |
  | 2024  | $52.5B | 15.0%    | $13.1B/qtr         |
  | 2025  | $91.4B | 22.7%    | $22.9B/qtr         |
  | Q1'26 | $35.7B | n/m      | $35.7B (annualized |
  |       |        |          |  $143B)            |
  +-------+--------+----------+--------------------+

  10-Q language: "we expect to significantly increase, relative to 2025,
  our investment in our technical infrastructure." Earnings release
  reiterated this. Wall Street consensus has clustered around $140-160B
  for FY26.

  Plus: $75.6B of NOT-YET-COMMENCED data center leases, beginning
  between 2026 and 2031 with non-cancelable terms 1-25 years. This is
  off-balance-sheet capex commitment that flows through opex (not
  capex) but represents real investment dollars.

  Plus: "multi-gigawatt TPU supply agreements" (Note 1, Q1'26 10-Q)
  with revenue beginning later in 2026, with the majority recognized
  in 2027. These are long-tail revenue commitments that BACK UP the
  capex demand signal -- customers paying GOOGL to operate
  multi-gigawatt-scale TPU farms.

  Plus: $40B private-company commitment ($10B funded, $30B
  contingent) -- not capex per se, but capital deployed.

3.2 CAPEX CURVE SCENARIOS

  +-------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+
  | Year  | Bull   | Base   | Bear   | Bull % | Base % | Bear % |
  |       |        |        |        | of rev | of rev | of rev|
  +-------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+
  | FY26  | $140B  | $148B  | $158B  | 28%    | 30%    | 33%    |
  | FY27  | $130B  | $150B  | $170B  | 22%    | 27%    | 33%    |
  | FY28  | $115B  | $140B  | $175B  | 17%    | 22%    | 30%    |
  | FY29  | $105B  | $125B  | $170B  | 14%    | 18%    | 27%    |
  | FY30  | $100B  | $115B  | $155B  | 12%    | 15%    | 23%    |
  | FY31  | $100B  | $110B  | $145B  | 11%    | 13%    | 20%    |
  | FY32  | $105B  | $115B  | $145B  | 11%    | 12%    | 19%    |
  | FY33+ | ~12% of revenue, growing with rev (steady state)        |
  +-------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+--------+

  Commentary on each scenario:

  BULL ($100-140B band, peaking FY26): TPU efficiency curve outpaces
  inference demand growth, allowing supply to catch up to demand by
  FY28. Revenue scales faster than capex; capex/revenue ratio
  normalizes by FY29. AI compute demand monetizes at high yields
  (TPU customers pay full-margin), justifying the FY26-FY27 build.

  BASE ($115-150B band, peaking FY27 then plateauing): Demand-pull
  matches capex through FY27 but begins to mature FY28-29. AI compute
  yields decline modestly as competition (NVIDIA, AMD MI400, custom
  silicon at AWS/Microsoft) intensifies. GOOGL maintains TPU edge in
  internal Gemini training and a meaningful slice of external Vertex/
  enterprise customers, justifying ~12% of revenue as steady-state
  capex. This is the AWS analog (AWS capex peaked at ~13% of segment
  revenue in 2023; ratio has been declining since).

  BEAR ($145-180B band, persisting through FY30): Capex demand sustains
  because competitive AI infra arms race continues; AND/OR demand for
  AI inference outstrips even our base; AND/OR returns on AI capex are
  poor and GOOGL is forced to keep investing to defend share. In this
  case, FCF is structurally suppressed for 5-7 years; net cash erodes;
  buybacks shrink; the IV compresses materially.

3.3 TPU AS THE CAPEX SWING FACTOR

  GOOGL is the only hyperscaler whose AI capex spend doesn't include

4. SEARCH TAM UNDER AI SUBSTITUTION
================================================================================

The bear thesis on GOOGL for the past 4 quarters has been that AI
chatbots (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Atlas) erode Search query share
and dilute the ad-supported business model.

The Q1 2026 print is the strongest empirical evidence we have that this
thesis is wrong, or at minimum slow-moving:
  - Search rev: +19% YoY (re-acceleration from ~13% FY25 average)
  - Paid clicks: +6% YoY (volume holding)
  - CPC: +7% YoY (pricing strong)
  - Pichai commentary: "queries at an all-time high"

But empirical evidence ≠ mechanism understanding. We should map the
mechanisms.

4.1 WHY SEARCH AI HASN'T (YET) CANNIBALIZED MONETIZATION

  Three mechanisms appear to be working in GOOGL's favor:

  (i) AI Overviews and AI Mode are MONETIZED SURFACES, not just
      product-feature additions. Sponsored results appear above and
      within AI Overview answers; AI Mode includes shopping ads. The
      "ads-with-AI-context" surface is structurally similar to the
      pre-AI sponsored search results, with arguably higher click
      intent.

  (ii) Increased query usage (Pichai's "all-time high"). Users
       perform MORE searches when AI assistance reduces the friction
       of complex multi-step queries. Aggregate ad inventory grows
       even if some queries get answered without a click.

  (iii) Subscription cross-sell. Google One (which now includes Gemini
        Advanced) is monetizing the "I want a chat-style answer" use
        case for $19.99/month at high gross margin. 350M paid
        subscribers (across YouTube + Google One) is growing rapidly.
        The chatbot-substitution-to-Search risk gets partially
        recaptured as subscription revenue.

4.2 QUANTIFYING SEARCH SUBSTITUTION SCENARIOS

  Search rev Q1'26:    $60.4B (annualized run-rate ~$240B if Q1

5. CAPITAL ALLOCATION & BUYBACK VELOCITY UNDER ASHKENAZI
================================================================================

Anat Ashkenazi joined as CFO in July 2024; 21 months in seat at end of
Q1 2026. Her first full fiscal year (FY25) saw $45.4B in buybacks
(retiring 240M shares) and the institution of a dividend ($0.20/qtr
launched 2024 -> $0.22/qtr by mid-2026). FY25 capital return was $55.4B,
or 76% of FCF.

5.1 THE Q1 2026 BUYBACK PAUSE

  Q1 2026 buybacks: $0 (vs. $15.1B Q1 2025)
  This is the first zero-buyback quarter for GOOGL in years.

  Three plausible explanations:

  (a) M&A SEQUENCING -- $33.6B in M&A closed Q1 (Wiz $29.5B + Intersect
      $5.9B), funded partially by $31.1B of new senior notes. Pausing
      buybacks during a quarter where you raise debt to fund M&A is
      sound capital-allocation hygiene -- avoids the optical and
      practical absurdity of "raise $31B in debt to repurchase $15B
      of equity."

  (b) CAPEX FRONT-LOADING -- $35.7B of Q1 capex (annualized $143B
      pace) consumes the operating cash flow that would normally fund
      buybacks. With OCF $45.8B in Q1 and capex $35.7B, FCF was only
      $10.1B; after dividends ($2.5B), only $7.6B left for buybacks
      -- and that gets eaten by working-capital uses.

  (c) STRUCTURAL SHIFT -- Ashkenazi prioritizes balance sheet
      preservation and M&A optionality over per-share value
      mechanics. This would be a meaningful negative shift for
      long-term shareholders if true.

  Our view: (a) and (b) are dominant; (c) is at most a 15-20% weight.
  Evidence:
    - $69.5B remaining buyback authorization (vs. ~$45B/yr historical
      run-rate = ~18 months of buyback runway)
    - Dividend was raised 5% in Q1 2026 ($0.21 -> $0.22)
    - Pichai/Ashkenazi commentary in past calls has emphasized capital
      return as a priority
    - The pause coincides exactly with the M&A close + debt issuance
      window

7.2 STRUCTURAL DRIVERS OF THE GAP TO AWS

  AWS advantages over GCP:
    - Scale (~50% more revenue today, ~3x more historical capex)

    - First-party AI silicon (TPU) -- AWS has Trainium/Inferentia
      but lower scale and less integration
    - First-party foundation model (Gemini) -- AWS does not own a
      frontier model; reliant on Anthropic and Bedrock partners
    - DeepMind / Research talent density
    - Lower opex per dollar of revenue at the data-center layer
      (better PUE / energy efficiency in newer regions)

  Net: structural reasons GCP can match or exceed AWS margin in
  steady state are real. The ~3-4pt gap today reflects the historical
  scale gap; we expect it to close to <2pt by FY28.

    - Cloud business returns to AWS-like 26-30% steady state but

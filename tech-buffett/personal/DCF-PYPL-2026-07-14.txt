SECTION 1 — BUSINESS SNAPSHOT & THE M&A SITUATION
================================================================================

PayPal operates a two-sided digital payments network (PayPal, Venmo,
Braintree, and PayPal-branded processing/value-added services) monetizing
via take rate on Total Payment Volume (TPV) plus interest/fee income on a
consumer credit and buy-now-pay-later (BNPL) loan book. FY2025 revenue mix:

  - Transaction revenue (take-rate on TPV):        $30,150M   90.9% of total
  - Revenue from other value-added services:         $3,022M    9.1% of total
    (partner revenue share, subscriptions, gateway fees, loan interest/fees)
  Total FY2025 revenue:                             $33,172M   +4.3% YoY

PayPal reports as a SINGLE operating segment today. Effective this quarter
(April 2026), new CEO Enrique Lores (former HP Inc. CEO, previously a PayPal
board member) reorganized the company into three internal business lines —
Checkout Solutions & PayPal; Consumer Financial Services & Venmo; Payment
Services & Crypto — with external segment reporting planned for "sometime
next year." See Section 3.

THE STRIPE/ADVENT PROPOSAL (breaking news, last 24 hours)
  - Reuters: Stripe and Advent International have offered $60.50/share,
    valuing PayPal at over $53 billion — a ~28% premium to the last
    undisturbed close ($47.37).
  - Structure reported: Stripe and Advent as equal co-owners, ~$50B of
    committed acquisition financing lined up.
  - Status: this followed discussions dating to "early April" 2026; a
    response from PayPal's board is still pending. This is NOT a signed
    merger agreement — treat it as a live, unresolved situation, not a
    completed transaction.
  - Strategic logic: Stripe is PayPal's most credible modern-era competitor
    in payment processing/infrastructure (the same competitive pressure
    referenced in PayPal's own PSP/Braintree commentary). A combination
    would be one of the largest fintech M&A transactions ever and would
    likely draw serious antitrust scrutiny given the two companies compete
    directly in payment processing for merchants.
  - Market reaction: the stock is trading meaningfully below $60.50 in
    early post-news action (~$53, based on the data available at the time
    of this analysis), which is the market pricing deal-completion risk
    (regulatory risk, financing risk, possibility the board rejects or
    renegotiates, possibility of a competing bid). This is a classic
    merger-arbitrage spread, not a fundamentals signal.

The thesis in three sentences (standalone, ignoring the deal):
  1. PayPal is a large, profitable, still-growing payments network (TPV +11%
     spot / +8% currency-neutral in Q1 2026) trading, pre-news, at a
     depressed multiple that reflected years of decelerating branded
     checkout growth, take-rate compression, and leadership uncertainty.
  2. A brand-new CEO has just launched a real reorganization (three focused
     business lines, simplified decision-making) paired with a disclosed
     $1.5B+ gross annualized cost-savings target over 2-3 years — a
     credible, quantified turnaround lever, not just a vague promise.
  3. Even without the turnaround working, PayPal throws off $6B+ of
     adjusted free cash flow a year against an ~$41-53B market cap
     (depending which price you use) and has been retiring ~8-9% of its
     share count annually through buybacks — a large, disciplined capital
     return program that compounds per-share value even in a low-growth
     scenario.

SECTION 3 — STRATEGIC CONTEXT: THE TURNAROUND UNDER A NEW CEO
================================================================================

Enrique Lores (former HP Inc. CEO, joined PayPal's board before taking the
CEO role) took over as President & CEO shortly before this earnings call —
his first as CEO. His framing, in his own words on the call: "our foundation
is strong... [but] we need to make significant changes to improve the
strategic and operational issues the company has faced."

KEY MOVES ANNOUNCED THIS QUARTER
  - Reorganization (announced April 29, 2026) into three business lines,
    each with a single accountable leader: Checkout Solutions & PayPal;
    Consumer Financial Services & Venmo; Payment Services & Crypto (which
    absorbs Braintree and the PSP/enterprise processing business, plus
    PYUSD stablecoin).
  - Cost program: "at least $1.5 billion of gross run-rate savings over the
    next two to three years," from (1) structural realignment/delayering
    and (2) "aggressive deployment of AI" in customer support, risk, and
    engineering. Management explicitly said a "vast majority" of the $1.5B
    comes from these two waves, phased over 2026-2028, with savings largely
    REINVESTED into growth rather than dropping fully to the bottom line —
    this is NOT a pure margin-expansion story in the near term.
  - Branded checkout (the crown jewel, highest-margin segment) is being
    treated as "the highest priority" — investments in presentment,
    top-50-merchant relationships, a new PayPal+ loyalty program (launched
    UK, expanding), and rebalancing focus back toward the consumer side of
    the network after years of merchant-side focus.
  - Stablecoin (PYUSD): "largest federally regulated stablecoin" as of
    December 2025, expanded to 70 markets — a call-out but not yet a
    disclosed material revenue line.

FY2026 GUIDANCE (qualitative, from the call — no explicit full-year revenue
or EPS dollar figure was given in the transcript provided, only ranges/
direction; treat the figures below as guidance color, not exact targets)
  - Full year: transaction margin dollars (ex-interest) roughly flat to
    slightly down; non-transaction opex +~3%; non-GAAP EPS "ranging from
    down low-single digits to slightly positive" — i.e., a roughly flat
    year at best on a per-share earnings basis, with the cost program not
    yet meaningfully net-accretive.
  - Q2 2026 (the toughest comp of the year): low-single-digit revenue
    growth (CC), transaction margin dollars down ~2-3%, non-GAAP EPS down
    ~9% YoY — a genuinely weak-looking quarter is already flagged and
    expected by management, driven by a large prior-year one-time partner
    benefit rolling off.
  - Reiterated capital return: ~$6B buybacks, "at least $6B" adjusted FCF
    for the year.

READ-THROUGH FOR THE MODEL: FY2026 is explicitly a trough/transition year
by management's own framing — margin compression continues near-term, and
the payoff from the reorg and cost program is a 2027-2028 story, not a 2026
one. The Base case below reflects this: modest revenue growth, only slow
margin recovery starting FY2027, with real margin expansion only showing up
from FY2028 onward as the savings program matures.

SECTION 4 — DCF ASSUMPTIONS
================================================================================

MODELING APPROACH
- 10-year explicit forecast: 2027E-2036E, off a FY2026E anchor built
  bottom-up from Q1 actual + qualitative full-year guidance color
- Unlevered FCF (FCFF): FCF = NOPAT + D&A - CapEx; "SBC-adjusted FCF"
  (subtracting stock-based comp as a real economic cost) is what's
  discounted and used for terminal value — consistent with the AMD model
  in this folder
- NOPAT = GAAP operating income x (1 - 20% effective tax rate)
- Net cash (not net debt) added to enterprise value: ~$1.9B per
  management's own Q1 2026 disclosure ($13.5B cash+investments vs $11.6B
  debt); the customer funds receivable/payable pair is excluded as
  operating float, not net cash
- SHARE COUNT: held FLAT at 900M (current-ish diluted count) throughout the
  forecast — NOT walked down for future buybacks. This is intentional, not
  an oversight: discounting all future FCF back to today's shareholders and
  ALSO shrinking the share count from that same future FCF would double-count
  the value of the buyback program. A standard DCF already assumes today's
  shareholders capture 100% of future cash flow, whether it arrives as
  dividends, buybacks, or retained earnings — this is the same convention
  used in the AMD model in this folder.
- Each scenario (Bull/Base/Bear) uses its own discount rate, terminal
  growth rate, and exit multiple, mirroring the CHTR/AMD DCFs in this folder

DISCOUNT RATE DERIVATION
PayPal carries real execution risk (a new CEO's first turnaround attempt,
competitive share pressure in its core checkout business, a genuinely
unresolved M&A situation overhanging the stock) but also a strong balance
sheet, real cash generation, and a scaled two-sided network that would be
very expensive for a challenger to replicate from scratch. This sits between
MSFT's 10% (used elsewhere in this portfolio) and AMD's 11.5%/13.5% — I use:
  BASE:  10.0%  (moderate execution risk, offset by balance sheet quality
         and real, already-visible cash generation)
  BULL:  8.5%   (turnaround de-risks itself with 2-3 quarters of proof;
         market re-rates PayPal back toward a quality-compounder multiple)
  BEAR:  12.0%  (structural share loss narrative confirmed; take-rate
         compression never stabilizes; reorg proves to be a distraction)

PROBABILITY WEIGHTS: Bull 25% / Base 45% / Bear 30% — same split used for
AMD, reflecting genuine two-sided uncertainty on whether a first-year CEO's
turnaround plan will work, balanced against a business that is already
solidly profitable and cash-generative even if nothing improves.

SECTION 7 — SCENARIO ANALYSIS
================================================================================

BULL CASE (~$135/share; 8.5% WACC, 3.0% TGR, 16x exit)
  - Enrique Lores's reorg works: branded checkout re-accelerates as
    presentment, loyalty (PayPal+), and top-merchant investments pay off
  - Venmo monetization continues scaling (already 6 consecutive quarters of
    double-digit TPV growth) and becomes a meaningfully larger profit pool
  - The $1.5B+ cost program becomes net-accretive to margin by 2028-2029
    (not just reinvested) as AI/automation savings materialize on schedule
  - Take-rate compression stabilizes as the Venmo/Enterprise Payments mix
    shift matures rather than continuing indefinitely
  - Market re-rates PayPal back toward a quality-compounder multiple as
    2-3 quarters of clean execution reduce perceived turnaround risk

BASE CASE (~$78/share; 10.0% WACC, 2.5% TGR, 13x exit)
  - Modest continuation of current trends: TPV keeps growing at a healthy
    high-single/low-double-digit clip, but take-rate compression eats most
    of the gap between TPV growth and revenue growth (similar to Q1 2026's
    TPV +11% vs. revenue +7%)
  - Cost savings materialize roughly as guided but are mostly reinvested
    near-term, with only gradual net margin improvement starting 2027-2028
  - Branded checkout stabilizes around flat-to-low-single-digit growth
    rather than reaccelerating meaningfully — "good enough," not "fixed"
  - Buybacks continue at a healthy (if not necessarily $6B/year for a
    full decade) pace, remaining the most reliable per-share value driver

BEAR CASE (~$38/share; 12.0% WACC, 1.5% TGR, 9x exit)
  - Branded checkout continues losing share structurally to Apple Pay,
    Google Pay, stablecoin-based rails, and BNPL-native competitors
    (Klarna, Affirm) — the multi-year erosion pattern that predates this
    CEO simply continues under new management
  - Enterprise Payments/Braintree pricing compresses under intensifying
    competition from Stripe and Adyen (notably, one of PayPal's most
    credible competitors is also the company reportedly trying to acquire
    it — a signal of how competitive this market has become)
  - The reorg proves to be a distraction rather than a catalyst: this would
    not be the first PayPal "reset" that didn't fully deliver
  - Take-rate compression never stabilizes as low-margin volume keeps
    growing faster than high-margin branded checkout
  - If the M&A situation collapses with no resolution and no competing
    bidder emerges, sentiment could sour further on standalone execution
    risk, reinforcing the depressed multiple rather than closing it

SECTION 8 — KEY RISKS
================================================================================

1. THE M&A SITUATION ITSELF IS THE DOMINANT NEAR-TERM RISK/CATALYST
   This is not a signed deal — it's a reported proposal with "a response
   still pending." Outcomes include: (a) PayPal's board accepts at $60.50
   or negotiates a higher price, (b) a competing bidder emerges given how
   cheap this model suggests PayPal is, (c) the board rejects the offer and
   the stock reverts toward something between the pre-news price and this
   model's standalone fair value, or (d) the deal is announced but faces
   protracted antitrust review (a horizontal combination of two major
   payment processors) and could ultimately be blocked. Any position taken
   on the stock right now is partly a bet on which of these plays out, on
   a timeline this DCF has no ability to predict.

2. EXECUTION RISK ON A BRAND-NEW CEO'S FIRST TURNAROUND (HIGH)
   Enrique Lores's plan is credible (clear framework, quantified cost
   target, reorganized accountability) but unproven — this is his first
   earnings call as CEO. PayPal has attempted prior resets that didn't
   fully restore growth. The market's historically skeptical pricing of
   PayPal (reflected in the pre-news ~16% implied discount rate) is not
   irrational given this history.

3. TAKE-RATE COMPRESSION (MEDIUM-HIGH, already in progress)
   Transaction take rate fell 6bps YoY to 1.62% in Q1 2026 as volume mix
   shifts toward lower-take-rate Venmo and Enterprise Payments/Braintree.
   This is a structural, not cyclical, dynamic — every incremental dollar
   of TPV growth from these channels is worth less than a dollar of legacy
   branded checkout volume. The Base and Bear cases both assume this
   continues to some degree; only Bull assumes it stabilizes.

4. COMPETITIVE INTENSITY (MEDIUM-HIGH)
   Apple Pay/Google Pay at the wallet layer, Stripe and Adyen in payment
   processing, Klarna/Affirm in BNPL, and emerging stablecoin-based rails
   all compete for pieces of PayPal's network simultaneously. The fact that
   Stripe — a direct competitor — is the reported acquirer underscores how
   contested this market has become.

5. FLAT ACTIVE ACCOUNT GROWTH (MEDIUM)
   Monthly active accounts grew only 1% YoY (225M) even as TPV grew 11%.
   Growth is coming entirely from existing users transacting more, not new
   user acquisition — a sustainable dynamic for a while, but a ceiling risk
   if it persists for years.

6. COST PROGRAM EXECUTION RISK (MEDIUM)
   The $1.5B+ savings target is disclosed and quantified, which is a
   credibility positive relative to vague turnaround promises, but
   management itself has not yet detailed the cadence — "we plan to share
   more details in the coming months." Multi-year cost programs at large
   companies frequently take longer and cost more (in restructuring
   charges) than initially guided.

7. INTEREST RATE SENSITIVITY (LOW-MEDIUM)
   A portion of "other value added services" revenue comes from interest
   earned on customer balances and the loan book; FY2025's growth in this
   line was partially offset by "lower revenues from interest earned...
   resulting from lower interest rates." Further rate cuts would be a
   modest headwind to this revenue line, independent of the operating
   turnaround.

8. REGULATORY (LOW-MEDIUM, both standalone and deal-related)
   PayPal operates a lending business (BNPL, PayPal/Venmo credit cards)
   and a stablecoin (PYUSD) in an evolving regulatory environment. A
   Stripe/PayPal combination specifically would combine two of the
   largest global payment processors — a serious antitrust question
   independent of the fintech-regulatory questions each company already
   navigates alone.

SECTION 9 — INVESTMENT ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

IS $60.50 A GOOD PRICE FOR SHAREHOLDERS?
Based on this standalone model: probably not obviously good, and arguably
light. Base case says PayPal is worth ~$78/share as an independent company
executing a plausible (not heroic) turnaround — 29% above the offer. Even
the Bear case, which assumes the turnaround largely fails and competitive
erosion continues indefinitely, produces $38/share — meaning the offer
already prices in a real amount of pessimism, but not the FULL bear case,
and nowhere close to the base or bull cases. The offer looks like a price
that's attractive relative to where the stock WAS trading (28% premium to
a genuinely depressed multiple) but not obviously full relative to what the
business can be worth if the new CEO's plan works even moderately well.

WHY THE MARKET IS STILL TRADING BELOW $60.50 RIGHT NOW
The reported ~$53 early post-news price versus a $60.50 offer is a normal
merger-arbitrage discount — it reflects deal-completion risk (no signed
agreement yet, antitrust risk given Stripe is a direct competitor,
financing risk on ~$50B of committed debt, and the general uncertainty of
an early-stage, reportedly unsolicited-or-preliminary proposal), not a
view that $60.50 is too rich. That spread compresses if/when a definitive
agreement is signed and widens further if the deal looks likely to collapse
or draw a blocking antitrust challenge.

POSITIVES (WHY THIS COULD STILL WORK OUT WELL FOR HOLDERS)
  + This model's base case, built conservatively off management's own
    "trough year" guidance, still clears both reference prices
  + Real, disclosed, quantified cost-savings program ($1.5B+) gives the
    turnaround thesis something concrete to hold management accountable to
  + Massive, sustained buyback program (~19% share count reduction in just
    over two years) compounds per-share value even in a low-growth outcome
  + Near-net-cash balance sheet — no leverage risk overhang, unlike a name
    like CHTR in this same folder
  + The M&A interest itself is a market signal: a sophisticated financial
    and strategic buyer with real competitive knowledge of the payments
    business is willing to pay a premium for it, which is at minimum a
    data point that "the business is broken" is not the consensus view
    among the people who understand it best

NEGATIVES (WHY CAUTION IS WARRANTED)
  - The standalone DCF conclusion (PayPal is undervalued) is exactly the
    kind of analysis that looks smart right up until a real acquirer
    validates it and then the situation resolves in a way this model can't
    forecast — deal timelines, negotiated final prices, and antitrust
    outcomes are not fundamentals questions
  - If the deal falls apart with no competing bid, the stock could revert
    toward something between the pre-news $47 and this model's fair value —
    a real gap remains even in that "bad" outcome, but the round-trip could
    be painful and slow
  - The turnaround thesis genuinely depends on an unproven, brand-new CEO's
    first attempt — history with PayPal turnarounds specifically warrants
    some humility here
  - Take-rate compression is a real, structural headwind already visible in
    the numbers, not a hypothetical risk

BOTTOM LINE
On fundamentals alone, PayPal screens as a reasonably priced-to-cheap
business even after a 28% takeover premium — a rare, useful, and slightly
unusual conclusion in a market where most quality names (see: the AMD
analysis in this folder) screen as expensive. But the fundamentals analysis
is now secondary to the deal dynamics: what matters most over the coming
weeks is whether PayPal's board accepts, rejects, or renegotiates, whether
a competing bidder appears (which this model's math would encourage), and
how regulators view a Stripe/PayPal combination. Treat this DCF as an
input to judging deal fairness, not as a standalone buy/sell signal on its
own — the situation is moving faster than any 10-year model can capture.

Conviction level on base case (standalone): MODERATE-HIGH
Conviction on near-term price direction: LOW — genuinely event-driven and
outside the scope of a DCF; monitor the news, not the model, for the next
several weeks.

  - extreme leverage ($118B pro forma net debt post Cox)

SECTION 1 — BUSINESS SNAPSHOT
================================================================================

Charter is the #2 US cable operator by subscribers (Spectrum brand), serving
~58M homes across 41 states. Revenue is driven by:

  - Residential broadband (Internet):  $23,765M  43.4% of total  +1.7% YoY
  - Mobile service (MVNO on Verizon):  $3,762M    6.9%          +22.0%
  - Video (linear, declining):          $13,703M  25.0%          -9.4%
  - Voice (VoIP, declining):            $1,350M    2.5%          -6.0%
  - Commercial (SMB + Enterprise):      $7,315M   13.4%          +0.9%
  - Advertising + Other:                $4,879M    8.9%          mixed
  -------------------------------------------------------------------
  Total FY2025 Revenue:                $54,774M                  -0.6%

The thesis in three sentences:
  1. Broadband is a near-monopoly infrastructure product — Charter has a
     fiber-powered HFC network passing 58M+ homes with no viable substitute
     at scale in most of its footprint.
  2. The CapEx supercycle (network evolution + rural buildout) ends in 2027-28,
     creating a massive FCF step-up: management guided standalone capex to
     <$8B by 2028, vs $11.7B in 2025 — a $3.7B/year improvement.
  3. The Cox acquisition ($12.6B assumed debt + $4B cash) adds ~6.5M customers,
     $12.5B of revenue, and $800M+ in run-rate synergies at a price management
     believes will prove very cheap in hindsight.

Key pending events (as of June 2026):
  - Cox transaction close: awaiting California PUC approval; management
    targets summer 2026. ~4/5 of Cox footprint approved; ~1/5 is CA.
  - Liberty Broadband merger: closing simultaneously with Cox.
  - Post-close Charter pro forma shares: ~179M fully diluted (as-converted).

SECTION 3 — COX TRANSACTION IMPACT
================================================================================

The Cox transaction fundamentally transforms Charter. Key terms:
  - Charter pays $4.0B cash to Cox Enterprises (financed with debt)
  - Charter assumes Cox's ~$12.6B net debt and finance leases
  - Charter issues $6.0B of convertible preferred units to Cox
    (6.875% dividend; converts at $477.41/unit)
  - Charter issues ~33.6M common units to Cox Enterprises
  - Simultaneously, Liberty Broadband merger reduces net shares by ~6.8M
  - Net new shares post-close: ~+39.2M Class A equivalent

Estimated Cox financials (private company; management color used):
  - Revenue: ~$12,500M (6.5M customers × ~$120/month ARPU × 12)
  - EBITDA margin: ~41% (CFO stated "not that different from Charter's")
  - EBITDA: ~$5,125M
  - Cox broadband ARPU is higher than Charter but customer ARPU is "not that
    different" — broadband pricing will be lowered; mobile/video penetration
    (very low at Cox) will drive ARPU preservation.
  - Run-rate opex synergies: $800M+ (guided; management expects more)
    Sources: programming (Cox + Charter combined leverage), procurement,
    overhead elimination, and operational convergence.
  - Integration CapEx: elevated in 2027-2028; Cox has good network (mid-split
    nearly complete; Charter can implement high-split/DOCSIS 4.0 at lower cost)

WACC DERIVATION
Cost of debt: 5.15% (management: weighted average cost of debt is 5.2%)
After-tax cost of debt: 5.15% × (1 – 22%) = 4.0%
Asset (unlevered) beta: 0.60 (cable/broadband infrastructure)
Capital structure (pro forma post-Cox): D/EV ~75%, E/EV ~25%
Levered beta: 0.60 × (1 + 0.78 × 75/25) = 0.60 × 3.34 = 2.00
Risk-free rate: 4.5% (10-year Treasury, June 2026)
Equity risk premium: 5.5%
Cost of equity: 4.5% + 2.0 × 5.5% = 15.5%
WACC: 0.75 × 4.0% + 0.25 × 15.5% = 3.0% + 3.9% = 6.9%
Rounded up to 7.0% mechanically; I use 9.0% as base case to be conservative
and reflect execution risk/leverage uncertainty premium.

Note on market-implied discount rate: Backing out the current stock price of
~$225 and pro forma EV of ~$156B implies the market is using a ~12% discount
rate — extremely pessimistic, embedding substantial execution risk and
leverage anxiety. My base case (9.0%) is between "fair" and "market implied."

SECTION 8 — KEY RISKS
================================================================================

1. LEVERAGE (HIGH)
   $94B current debt ($118B post-Cox). At 4.15x Adjusted EBITDA with no
   margin of safety if EBITDA misses. Even a 10% EBITDA miss would put Charter
   at 4.6x leverage — above target range. Interest costs are $5B/year and rising.

2. BROADBAND SUBSCRIBER LOSSES (HIGH)
   Charter has lost ~1.5M Internet subscribers over the past two years.
   Q1 2026 loss of 120K continues the trend. Competition:
     - AT&T fiber now at 27% of Charter footprint
     - Verizon at 16%
     - Fixed wireless: T-Mobile, AT&T now filling FWA where fiber isn't built
     - Mobile substitution still growing
   Revenue per customer is partly offsetting (ARPU +1-2%), but volume
   matters for long-term pricing power.

3. COX EXECUTION RISK (MEDIUM-HIGH)
   Integrating a ~$12.5B revenue cable system is a multi-year project. Key risks:
     - California PUC approval: still pending; delay or conditions possible
     - Cox broadband ARPU compression during Spectrum rebrand transition
     - Customer reaction to brand change + pricing restructuring
     - Field ops insourcing is slower and costlier than expected
   Charter has done this before (TWC, Bright House) so mgmt confidence is
   somewhat justified, but Cox is larger than any single prior integration.

4. MOBILE MARGINS (MEDIUM)
   Spectrum Mobile uses Verizon's network as MVNO. Economics are positive but
   thin. At 12M lines, mobile direct costs are a margin drag. Path to profitability:
     - Growing CBRS/WiFi offload to reduce Verizon payments
     - New T-Mobile agreement for business customers (launches 2026)
     - Scale: mobile EBITDA neutral at ~10-12M lines; positive beyond
   Verizon renegotiation risk: if Verizon raises MVNO pricing, Charter's
   economics deteriorate. This is a key overhang.

5. VIDEO (LOW/MANAGED)
   Video continues to decline (-9.4% in 2025). Charter is managing this by:
     - Including streaming apps within video packages (Seamless Entertainment)
     - Moving to Xumo stream boxes for new video customers
     - Reducing programming costs as video mix shifts lighter
   Video is becoming less important (from 27% of revenue to ~20% by 2028E).
   The risk is that video churn pulls broadband customers with it.

6. REGULATORY / POLITICAL (LOW-MEDIUM)
   - Net neutrality could be reimposed (FCC currently treats broadband as
     "information service" — favorable; Democratic administration could reverse)
   - Franchise fee legislation
   - Rural buildout obligations with milestones tied to RDOF subsidies
   - California-specific: PUC is the current bottleneck for Cox close

7. CAPEX OVERRUN RISK (MEDIUM)
   Charter guided "below $8B standalone by 2028." If network evolution
   takes longer than expected or Cox network requires more investment,
   the FCF cliff could be delayed 12-24 months — meaningful given the
   leverage and the market's focus on near-term FCF.

8. SATELLITE / LEO (LOW NEAR-TERM)
   Management says Starlink not yet showing meaningful share loss. In low-density
   rural areas, satellite is actually complementary. Charter sees potential to
   resell/bundle satellite. Not an existential threat at current pricing levels
   for suburban/urban markets, but worth monitoring in 3-5 year horizon.

SECTION 9 — INVESTMENT ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

POSITIVES (WHY IT COULD BE A BUY)
  + Deeply undervalued on FCF yield: ~25%+ FCF yield by 2028 at current price
    (per management's own calculation, consistent with my model)
  + CapEx cliff is the biggest near-term catalyst: $11.7B → <$8B standalone
    by 2028 is not speculative — the network evolution is mostly funded
  + Infrastructure moat: Charter's fiber-powered HFC network passes 58M homes;
    nobody is building a 4th broadband wire to most of these homes
  + Mobile growing at 17% YoY; now 12M lines; EBITDA drag is temporary
  + Cox is essentially a free call option on synergies — management has
    done this before and the $800M synergy number seems conservative
  + Aggressive share buybacks: ~$3.8B in 2025, $963M in Q1 2026 alone
    at $225/share — buybacks at these levels are strongly accretive
  + Charter is buying back COX preferred at effectively deep discount vs
    the intrinsic value management believes the business is worth
  + Nick Jeffrey (ex-Verizon, ex-EE) joining as COO brings competitive
    wireless market experience that Charter has lacked

NEGATIVES (WHY IT COULD BE A TRAP)
  - $118B of pro forma debt is not forgiving of execution errors
  - Broadband unit losses have not yet bottomed; no clear inflection visible
  - Cox close timing uncertain (California PUC); if 2026 close becomes 2027,
    near-term FCF is worse and leverage stays elevated longer
  - Mobile economics depend on Verizon relationship; concentration risk
  - The stock has already declined significantly from highs ($750+);
    investors who caught the thesis early are sitting on large losses
  - "Cheap" on FCF yield has been the Charter thesis for 2+ years;
    execution on the capex cliff is still not proven

BOTTOM LINE
Charter is a levered bet on broadband infrastructure at a market price
that implies 12% discount rate — the same you'd apply to a distressed company.
Charter is not distressed; it generates $5B/year in FCF today and nearly
twice that by 2028. The discount is driven by:
  (a) legitimate execution risk on Cox integration
  (b) ongoing subscriber headwinds that have hurt management credibility
  (c) leverage-induced skittishness

If Charter executes on even 80% of its stated plan — CapEx cliff happens,
